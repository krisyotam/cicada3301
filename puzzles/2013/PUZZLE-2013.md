# 2013 Puzzle — The Second Round

**Posted**: January 4, 2013

## Overview

The second puzzle introduced several elements that would become central to Cicada 3301's identity: the Gematria Primus cipher, the bootable Cicada OS, and "The Instar Emergence" music file.

## Key Developments

### Cicada OS (3301.iso)

A bootable Linux ISO (~130MB) based on Tiny Core Linux was discovered as part of the puzzle chain. It contained:
- Audio files with steganographic content
- The message: "@1231507051321 The key is all around you. Good luck. 3301"
- Boot sequence displaying "1033" and "3301"

Available at: [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/3301.iso)

### Gematria Primus

The puzzle introduced the **Gematria Primus** — a custom cipher system mapping 29 Anglo-Saxon (Elder Futhark) runes to prime numbers. This became the foundational encoding system for Liber Primus.

Each rune corresponds to:
- A letter or digraph (e.g., ᚠ = F, ᚦ = TH, ᛝ = NG)
- A prime number value
- A position in the sequence

Full reference: [Uncovering Cicada Wiki — Gematria Primus](https://uncovering-cicada.fandom.com/wiki/Gematria_Primus)

### The Instar Emergence (761.mp3)

A guitar track with reversed and distorted elements:
- **Duration**: 2:47 (167 seconds — 167 is prime and the emirp of 761)
- **Gematria value**: "The Instar Emergence" sums to 761 in Gematria Primus
- **ID3 tag hidden poem**: "Parable 1,595,277,641 / Like the instar, tunneling to the surface / We must shed our own circumferences; / Find the divinity within and emerge."

Available at: [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/cicada-761)

### Telnet Hidden Service

A Tor hidden service accessible via telnet at `emiwp4muu2ktwknf.onion`. Participants were instructed to:
1. Create their own Tor hidden services with CGI file upload capability
2. Post magic squares and their .onion URLs
3. Each participant received unique numeric encryption puzzles designed to detect and exclude information leakers

The telnet prompt was later recreated by the community at `dontquitownit.com:3301`.

## Winner

**Marcus Wanner**, age 15, solved the 2013 puzzle. Winners were invited to an anonymous chat server and tasked with building a **"dead man's switch"** for whistleblowers — a system that would automatically release information if the holder failed to check in.

The project eventually fell apart due to internal disagreements and coordination difficulties among the anonymous participants.
